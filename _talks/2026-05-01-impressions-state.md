**Abstract:** Sealing practices in ancient Egypt were used in a variety of administrative and religious contexts. Though evidence for the use of seals can be found at all periods in ancient Egyptian history, seal impressions found at Middle Kingdom sites indicate a significant expansion of the administrative sealing system during the early second millennium BCE. Studies of Egyptian sealing practices have historically focused on iconography, including my previous analysis of the material, though an integration of quantitative analysis is necessary for a more comprehensive understanding of the available data. This paper presents an exploratory quantitative analysis of the physical and spatial properties of seal impressions from the Egyptian colonial site of Elephantine during the late Middle Kingdom (ca. 1850–1630 BCE) to gain further insight into sealing practices on Elephantine to investigate the status of individuals, administrative activities of site, and distribution of import versus locally produced goods. This data provides key insights into aspects of daily life on Elephantine and about material discard practices there. Due to Elephantine’s strategic location on the southern border of the Egyptian Empire, this study seeks to analyze regional trends both inside of and outside of the Egyptian heartland.
