**Abstract:** Sealing practices in ancient Egypt were used in a variety of administrative and religious contexts. Though evidence for the use of seals can be found at all periods in ancient Egyptian history, seal impressions found at Middle Kingdom sites indicate a significant expansion of the administrative sealing system during the early second millennium B.C.E. This expansion was, in large part, a result of colonial expansion into Lower Nubia, and was accompanied by the establishment of fortresses along Egypt's new southern border between the Second and Third Cataracts. This paper presents an exploratory comparative analysis of iconography and a qualitative analysis of the physical and spatial properties of seal impressions, including physical size, distribution across the site, and provenance, from domestic and administrative contexts at two contemporary Egyptian colonial sites: Elephantine and Askut. The goal of this analysis is to gain further insight into sealing practices in colonial zones during the late Middle Kingdom (ca. 1850-1630 B.C.E.), which can potentially tell us about individual status, administrative activities, and production of goods at, and potential interaction between, both sites. This data provides key insights into regional trends and variation of sealing practices, as well as expressions of identity within the confines of imperial bureaucracy.
